Crossproduct + Eigensolver

In the tall/skinny case where $m>n$, this is calculated by taking the eigenvalue decomposition of $A^TA$, or the “crossproducts matrix”. This matrix will be $n\times n$ (and so if $n$ is small, the matrix is small). The square root of its eigenvalues are the singular values of $A$, and its eigenvectors are the right singular vectors $V$ of $A$. The left singular vectors $U$ can be recovered by multiplying $A$ on the right by $V\Sigma^{-1}$.

Likewise, if $m<n$, then we can instead use $AA^T$ as our “crossproducts matrix”. The eigenvalues of this matrix are again the square of the singular values of $A$, only now its eigenvectors are the left singular vectors $U$ of $A$.

A nice thing about this approach is that it’s pretty quick

gesvda.

The ‘a’ in the function name is for “approximate”. There are worse names you could give it, although it’s not “approximate” in the sense of truncated SVD approximations. The issue is that by forming the crossproducts matrix, you are squaring the condition number, which is potentially a big deal for the numerical accuracy of the eigensolver. In practice, this is generally less of a big deal than the numerical mathematicians like to pretend.

This is the same as fml’s linalg::cpsvd() except that gesvda requires $m>n$ (and of course, fml’s works on CPU as well).

QR + SVD

Older versions of CUDA (pre 10.2) did not correctly handle non-square matrices in gesvd(), which could lead to OOM-like scenarios for even small matrices. And at the time of writing, it does not handle the $m<n$ case. For either of these problems, we can use a QR (or LQ) to reduce the problem to a square matrix, and then call gesvd().

If $m>n$, then

$$ \begin{align*} A &= QR \\
&= \left(Q U_R\right) \Sigma V^T \end{align*} $$

You can easily verify that in this case, $\Sigma$ is the singular values of both $A$ and $R$, and likewise the right singular vectors $V^T$. The left singular vectors of $A$ are $U=Q U_R$, where $U_R$ is the left singular vectors of $R$.

Similarly if $m<n$ we can use the LQ factorization

$$ \begin{align*} A &= LQ \\
&= U \Sigma \left(V_L^T Q\right) \end{align*} $$

cuSOLVER provides a QR but not an LQ. In fml, we use cuSOLVER’s QR on a transpose.
